# PART 1: EVERYDAY EXPRESSIONS (일상 표현)

## 1.1 Training & Preparation Expressions

### "Just 3 km."

**Original Korean:** "그냥 3km."

**Nuance:** A short, easy run before a major event (often called a "tapering run" in running)

**Context:**
When training for a major race, runners do very light runs (called "taper") the final days to preserve energy. "Just 3 km" emphasizes how minimal and easy this run is compared to the training leading up to it.

**Variations by Context:**

| Expression | Meaning | Intensity |
|------------|---------|-----------|
| "Just 3 km." | 그냥 3km | ★☆☆☆☆ |
| "Easy 3 km." | 쉬운 3km | ★☆☆☆☆ |
| "A quick 3 km." | 빠른 3km | ★★☆☆☆ |
| "A 3 km run." | 3km 달리기 | ★★☆☆☆ |

**Related Expressions:**
- "Light run" = 가벼운 달리기
- "Recovery run" = 회복 달리기 (근육 회복을 위한 가벼운 달리기)
- "Shakeout run" = 준비 달리기 (경기 전 몸 풀기)

**Practice Dialogue:**
```
A: Are you running today?
B: Just 3 km. I'm saving energy for the race.
A: Smart. Don't overdo it.
B: That's the plan. Keeping it light.
```

**Context:** Athletes do this regularly, especially before major events.

**Original Korean:** "최고 컨디션."

**Nuance:** At your absolute best physically; optimal readiness

**Intensity Spectrum (from worst to best):**

| Expression | Physical State | Rating |
|------------|---|---|
| "I feel terrible." | 형편없다 | ★☆☆☆☆ |
| "I feel okay." | 괜찮다 | ★★☆☆☆ |

**Similar Expressions:**
- "I'm at my best." (최고 상태다)
- "I'm in top form." (최고 형태다)

## 1.2 Confirmation & Agreement Expressions

### "Understood." / "Okay."

**Original Korean:** "알겠습니다." / "좋습니다."

**Nuance:** Brief acknowledgment; showing compliance with instructions

**Context:** Doyun gives short, respectful responses to his teacher's advice

**Formality Levels:**

| Expression | Formality | Context |
|------------|-----------|---------|
| "Okay." | Casual | 친구, 가족 |
| "Got it." | Casual | 일상 |
| "Sure." | Neutral | 중간 |
| "Understood." | Formal | 존댓말 (상사/선생님) |
| "I understand." | Very Formal | 공식 상황 |
| "Roger that." | Military/Casual slang | 특수 상황 |

**When Used in Conversations:**

```
Teacher: "Take Friday and Saturday off."
Student: "Okay."  ← casual but respectful

Boss: "Send the report by Friday."
Employee: "Understood."  ← formal acknowledgment

Friend: "Let's meet at 5."
You: "Got it!"  ← casual agreement
```

**Practice Dialogue:**
```
Coach: "Rest for two days before the race."
Runner: "Understood."
Coach: "And eat well."
Runner: "Of course."
Coach: "Come to the starting line at peak condition."
Runner: "I will."
```

# PART 2: PHYSICAL SENSATIONS (신체 감각 표현)

## 2.1 Positive Physical Sensations

### "My legs feel light."

**Original Korean:** "다리가 가볍다."

**Nuance:** Legs are energized, responsive, not fatigued

**This is a POSITIVE sign in running:**
- Muscles are rested
- No soreness or heaviness
- Ready to perform
- Energy reserves are full

**Contrasting Physical States:**

| Expression | Meaning | Training Stage |
|------------|---------|---|
| "My legs feel heavy." | 다리가 무겁다 | After hard training; fatigued |
| "My legs feel tight." | 다리가 경직되어 있다 | Sore; needs stretching |
| "My legs feel numb." | 다리가 저리다 | Long run aftermath |
| "My legs feel light." | 다리가 가볍다 | Well-rested; ready to run |
| "My legs feel fresh." | 다리가 싱싱하다 | Fully recovered; energized |
| "My legs feel strong." | 다리가 강하다 | Good power; capable |

**Body Part Vocabulary for Runners:**

| Body Part | Common Sensations |
|-----------|---|
| Legs | light, heavy, tight, sore, fresh, strong |
| Lungs/Chest | open, restricted, tight, clear, burning |
| Heart | racing, steady, pounding, calm |
| Mind | clear, foggy, focused, scattered |

**Practice Dialogue:**
```
Coach: How do your legs feel?
Runner: They feel light. Really light.
Coach: That's exactly what we want.
Runner: I'm ready to go.
Coach: Perfect. Save that energy for Sunday.
```

---

### "Breathing is smooth."

**Original Korean:** "호흡이 편하다."

**Nuance:** No difficulty breathing; smooth oxygen flow; controlled breathing rhythm

**Why This Matters in Running:**
- Sign of good cardiovascular fitness
- Indicates proper aerobic zone (not pushing too hard)
- Shows confidence and comfort
- Essential for long distances like marathons

**Breathing States During Exercise:**

| Breathing State | Description | Effort Level |
|---|---|---|
| "Breathing is heavy." | 숨이 차다 | Very high effort |
| "Breathing is labored." | 숨이 가쁘다 | High effort |
| "Breathing is quickened." | 숨이 빨라진다 | Moderate-high effort |
| "Breathing is steady." | 호흡이 안정적이다 | Moderate effort |
| "Breathing is smooth." | 호흡이 부드럽다 | Controlled; aerobic |

**Why Runners Monitor Breathing:**

```
Smooth breathing = sustainable pace
Labored breathing = too fast/unsustainable
Controlled breathing = good form
Uncontrolled breathing = panic/fatigue
```

**Practice Dialogue:**
```
A: I'm worried about the marathon. I might run out of breath.
B: You won't if your breathing is smooth from the start.
A: How do I keep it smooth?
B: Control your pace. Run at a conversational speed.
```

## 4.2 Gear & Preparation Phrasal Verbs

### "Doubled-checked"

**Meaning:** 한 번 더 확인했다; 재확인했다

**Original Sentence:** "I double-checked my gear."

**Structure:** double (adverb) + check (verb)
- Do something two times to be sure
- Common in preparation contexts

**Variations by Checking Method:**

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning | When Used |
|---|---|---|
| "check" | 확인하다 | Basic check |
| "double-check" | 재확인하다 | Second verification |
| "triple-check" | 세 번 확인하다 | Very careful |
| "check twice" | 두 번 확인하다 | Standard verification |
| "go over" | 검토하다 | Detailed review |

**What Athletes Double-Check Before Races:**

```
□ Shoes (are they broken in? laces tight?)
□ Clothes (layers appropriate for weather?)
□ Race bib (number readable? pinned correctly?)
□ Timing chip (attached to shoe/wrist?)
□ Nutrition (gels, water, electrolytes?)
□ Route map (do I know the course?)
□ Start time (when exactly do I need to be there?)
□ Hotel/accommodation (confirmed?)
```

**Practice Sentences:**

```
1. "I double-checked my gear before the race."
2. "Make sure to double-check your shoes are tied."
3. "She double-checked the time three times."
4. "We always double-check the route before running."
5. "I double-checked everything and felt ready."
```

# PART 5: CONVERSATIONAL PATTERNS (회화 패턴)

## 5.1 Check-In & Status Reporting Patterns

### Pattern 1: "How did it go?"

**Structure:** How + past tense question about recent event

**From Text:**
```
Teacher: "I did my final run today."
Teacher: "How did it go?"
```

**Uses:** Asking about the result/feeling of a recent activity

**5+ Examples:**

| Activity | How Did It Go? | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Run | "How did your run go?" | "Great! Felt strong." |
| Test | "How did the test go?" | "Pretty well, I think." |
| Interview | "How did your interview go?" | "Better than expected." |
| Date | "How did the date go?" | "Really nice, actually." |
| First day | "How did your first day go?" | "Overwhelming, but good." |

**Alternative Structures:**

```
"How did it go?" (most common)
"How'd it go?" (very casual)
"How was it?" (more open-ended)
"How did that work out?" (slightly formal)
"How did things go?" (neutral)
```

**Practice Dialogue:**

```
A: Did you do your taper run?
B: Yeah, this morning.
A: How did it go?
B: Perfect. Legs felt light, breathing was smooth.
A: That's exactly what you need.
B: I know. I'm feeling confident.
```

---

### Pattern 2: "You're all set." + Instruction Pattern

**Structure:** [Positive Assessment] + [Directive for Future]

**From Text:**
```
Teacher: "Perfect. Your legs are light and breathing is smooth."
Teacher: "Great. You're all set."
Teacher: "Take Friday and Saturday off."
Teacher: "Come out Sunday morning at peak condition."
```

**5+ Examples:**

| Assessment | Instruction | Context |
|---|---|---|
| "You're all set." | "Take it easy before the race." | Athletic |
| "That sounds good." | "Keep it up for two more weeks." | Training |
| "Perfect." | "Make sure to sleep well." | Health |
| "You're ready." | "Go show them what you've got." | Encouragement |
| "Looking good." | "Don't forget your water bottle." | Preparation |

**Practice Dialogue:**

```
Coach: "Show me your training log."
Athlete: [shows log]
Coach: "Excellent work. You're all set."
Athlete: "What should I do this week?"
Coach: "Rest. Save your energy for Sunday."
```

---

### Pattern 3: "Come [action] [time]" - Directive Pattern

**Structure:** Come + verb phrase + [when]

**From Text:**
```
"Come out Sunday morning at peak condition."
"Come tell me when you finish the race."
```

**This is Encouraging/Requesting Language:**

| Example | Speaker | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| "Come tell me when you finish." | Grandpa | 끝났을 때 나한테 와서 말해 |
| "Come out Sunday morning." | Teacher | 일요일 아침에 최고 상태로 나타나 |

| "Come find me after." | Coach | 끝나고 나를 찾아와 |

**More Examples with "Come":**

```
"Come eat lunch with us." (우리와 점심 먹어)
"Come help me with this." (이거 도와줘)
"Come see what I found." (내가 찾은 거 봐)

```

**Practice Dialogue:**

```

```

## 5.2 Reflection & Summarization Patterns

### Pattern 4: Time + Progression + Summary

**Structure:** [Starting point] + [Intermediate steps] + [Current state]

**From Text:**
```
"One year of training.
Started in March with bus sickness.
Walking, running, a 10km race, a half-marathon.
And now. A full marathon."
```

**This Pattern Shows:**
- Journey/progression
- Cumulative effort
- Building to climax

**5+ Examples with This Pattern:**

| Starting Point | Middle Steps | Current State |
|---|---|---|
| Didn't speak English | Took classes, practiced daily | Now fluent |
| Weighing 200 lbs | Ate healthier, exercised | Now 160 lbs |
| Never traveled | Visited 3 countries | Planning my 4th trip |
| Failed my first test | Studied hard, improved | Now top of class |
| Scared of heights | Climbed small walls | About to summit Everest |

**Using Time Anchors:**

```
"One year ago, I couldn't run at all.
Six months ago, I did my first 5K.
Three months ago, I ran a half-marathon.
Now, I'm ready for a full marathon."
```

**Practice Speaking:**

