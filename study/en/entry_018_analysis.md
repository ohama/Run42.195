### Variations & Alternatives

**For "Once you get used to something, you don't feel it anymore":**
- "Familiarity breeds indifference"
- "You get numb to it" (more informal)
- "You lose the initial excitement" (more specific)
- "Habituation" (noun form - scientific)

**For "It feels like enough, but also like something's missing":**
- "It's satisfying yet unsatisfying"
- "I'm content but restless"
- "It's good but there's something more I need"
- "It seems complete yet incomplete"

### Practice Dialogue

**Dialogue 1: A parent noticing their child's change**
```
Parent: You seem distracted lately. Everything okay?
Doyun: Yeah, I guess. Walking to school has just become routine, you know?
Parent: That's actually a good thing. It means it's not hard anymore.
Doyun: I know, but I also feel like I'm missing something. The excitement.
Parent: That's normal. You adapt to things. Once you get used to something, you don't feel it anymore.
Doyun: That's exactly what Grandpa said!
```

**Dialogue 2: Talking to a friend about progress**
```
Minseo: Hey, how long are you gonna keep walking?
Doyun: I don't know. Never thought about it.
Minseo: Until graduation? Even in high school?
Doyun: No idea. I don't even know which high school I'll go to.
Minseo: Fair point. But hey, you've gotten pretty good at running too.
Doyun: Really? I didn't notice.
```

**Dialogue 3: Internal reflection becomes external discussion**
```
Friend: You look thoughtful. What's up?
Doyun: I've been thinking about whether walking is enough.
Friend: What do you mean?
Doyun: It used to be exciting, but now it's just routine. No particular thoughts.
Friend: Maybe that means you're ready for the next step?
Doyun: Maybe... but I don't know what that step would be.
```

### Common Mistakes

| ❌ Incorrect | ✓ Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| "I stop to notice it" | "I stop noticing it" | Habituation = losing awareness, not pausing |
| "It feels like enough but missing" | "It feels like enough, but also like something's missing" | The "also" clarifies the contradiction |
| "Walking was too familiar" | "Walking has become too familiar" | Present perfect = recent change state |
| "I got used to smell it" | "Once you get used to smelling it" | Gerund after "get used to" |
| "What's next? I need to think." | "What's next?" (pause) | Rhetorical questions in diary can stand alone |

## PART 4: PHRASAL VERBS (구동사)

### Phrasal Verbs from Entry 018

| Phrasal Verb | Korean | Original Meaning | Usage in Text | Alternative |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **"Got used to"** (get + used to) | 익숙해지다 | Become accustomed | "Once you get used to something, you don't feel it anymore" | Accustomed to, familiarized with |
| **"Turned out well"** (turn + out) | 잘 나오다, 잘되다 | Resulted positively | "The blooms turned out well this year" | Worked out, came out well |
| **"Hit my nose"** (hit + direct object) | 코를 때리다/냄새가 났다 | Strike suddenly | "It hit my nose" (scent) | Struck, reached, caught |
| **"Filled my lungs"** (fill + object) | 폐를 채우다 | Occupied completely | "The sweet scent filled my lungs" | Occupied, flooded |
| **"Stopped noticing"** (stop + gerund) | 더 이상 못 느낀다 | Ceased the action | "You stop noticing" | Quit noticing, ceased to notice |
| **"Ran the whole thing"** (run + object) | 끝까지 뛰다 | Completed by running | "You actually ran the whole thing" | Completed, finished running |
| **"Stop and walk"** (stop + action) | 멈춰서 걷다 | Pause activity to do another | "Used to stop and walk halfway" | Pause and switch |
| **"Keep walking"** (keep + gerund) | 계속 걷다 | Continue the action | "How long are you gonna keep walking?" | Continue, persist in |
| **"Gotten good at"** (get + adjective + at) | ~을 잘하게 되다 | Become skilled | "You've gotten pretty good at running" | Become skilled, improved in |

### Breaking Down Phrasal Verbs

**"Get used to" Structure:**
```
GET USED TO + NOUN/GERUND
- "get used to something" (habituation)
- "get used to doing something" (habituation to action)

Examples from life:
- Get used to the commute
- Get used to running
- Get used to the smell
- Get used to waking up early
```

**Comparing Similar Verbs:**

| Verb | Similar Meaning | Nuance | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Get used to | Become accustomed to | Active process of adaptation | "I got used to walking" |
| Get familiar with | Become knowledgeable about | Learning/understanding | "I got familiar with the route" |
| Become accustomed to | Become accustomed to | More formal; passive state | "I became accustomed to walking" |
| Adjust to | Adapt to | Active change/modification | "I adjusted to waking early" |

## PART 5: CONVERSATIONAL PATTERNS (회화 패턴)

### Patterns from Entry 018

#### Pattern 1: Questioning with "How long... gonna... ?"

**Structure:**
```
"How long are you gonna [VERB]?"
"How long are you gonna keep [VERB + -ing]?"
"How long are you gonna [CONTINUE ACTION]?"
```

**Usage:** Asking about duration or long-term commitment

**Examples from text:**
- "How long are you gonna keep walking?" (Minseo's question)
- "How long are you gonna stay in this hobby?"
- "How long are you gonna do PE class?"

**Similar structures:**
- "For how much longer will you...?" (more formal)
- "When are you gonna stop...?" (more direct)
- "Are you planning to keep...?" (more polite)

**Dialogue example:**
```
Minseo: How long are you gonna keep walking?
Doyun: I honestly haven't thought about it.
Minseo: Would you do it through high school?
Doyun: No idea. I don't even know which high school I'll go to.
```

---

#### Pattern 2: Expressing Uncertainty - "I don't know... never thought about it"

**Structure:**
```
"I don't know..." → [Optional expansion]
"Never thought about it" / "Never thought about it before"
"Haven't thought about that" (present perfect)
```

**Usage:** Admitting lack of prior consideration or future planning

**Examples from text:**
- "I don't know... never thought about it."
- "I don't know which high school I'll go to."
- "I don't even know which high school I'll go to."

**Responses that acknowledge this:**
- "That's fair/true"
- "Makes sense"
- "Yeah, that's a good point"

**Dialogue example:**
```
Friend: Did you think about continuing running after graduation?
Doyun: I don't know. Never thought about it.
Friend: Would you want to?
Doyun: Maybe? I don't even know my high school plans yet.
```

---

#### Pattern 3: Expressing Contradiction - "But... also..."

**Structure:**
```
"[Statement 1]. But also [Contrasting Statement 2]."
"It feels like [POSITIVE], but also like [NEGATIVE]."
"It's [EMOTION], but I also feel [DIFFERENT EMOTION]."
```

**Usage:** Expressing mixed feelings or internal conflict

**Examples from text:**
- "It feels like enough, but also like something's missing."
- "It's good, but I also feel like I'm missing something."
- "It's not hard anymore, but..."

**Emotional spectrum shown:**
```
Contentment + Restlessness = Emotional tension
Satisfaction + Incompleteness = Internal conflict
Progress + Uncertainty = Growth moment
```

**Dialogue example:**
```
Friend: You should be happy. Walking is easier now.
Doyun: I am happy. But I also feel like I'm missing something.
Friend: Like what?
Doyun: The excitement, I guess. It's become too routine.
Friend: That's actually a sign you're ready to grow.
```

---

#### Pattern 4: Rhetorical Questions - Thinking Out Loud

**Structure:**
```
"Is that good or bad?"
"What's next?"
"Should I just...?"
"Maybe [OPTION]?"
```

**Usage:** Internal dialogue; self-questioning; considering options

**Examples from text:**
- "Is that good or bad?"
- "What's next?"
- "Should I just keep walking like this?"
- "Maybe just part of it?"

**Function in diary writing:**
- Shows internal conflict
- Engages the reader (even though reader is the writer)
- Breaks up narrative flow
- Demonstrates thinking process

**Dialogue example:**
```
Doyun: (sitting alone) Is walking enough anymore? What's next?
Friend: (overhearing) Why the serious face?
Doyun: Just thinking about whether I should push myself harder.
Friend: Like how?
Doyun: Maybe try running? I don't know.
```

---

#### Pattern 5: Building a Logical Argument - "If... then... maybe..."

**Structure:**
```
"If [CONDITION], then [CONSEQUENCE]."
"If [CONDITION], maybe [POSSIBILITY]."
"So [LOGICAL CONCLUSION]?"
```

**Usage:** Reasoning through decisions; building arguments

**Examples from text:**
- "If walking's become too easy, maybe try running."
- "If I can run six laps now, then maybe I can run part of my commute."
- "So why not try running?"

**Thought progression:**
```
OBSERVATION: Walking has become routine
↓
PAST FACT: I can now run six laps without stopping
↓
LOGICAL QUESTION: So why not try running part of the commute?
↓
TENTATIVE CONCLUSION: Maybe worth thinking about
```

**Dialogue example:**
```
Minseo: You can run long distances now, right?
Doyun: Yeah, but that's different from my commute.
Minseo: How? If you ran six laps, why not run part of 7 km?
Doyun: It's a lot more than six laps.
Minseo: But you've also gotten stronger. So maybe it's possible?
Doyun: Maybe... I hadn't thought of it that way.
```

### Expressing Mixed/Conflicted Emotions

**Pattern for internal conflict:**
```
[Positive emotion] + [Negative emotion] = Growth moment

Contentment ("It's not hard") + Restlessness ("Missing something")
= Readiness for challenge

Achievement ("Ran six laps") + Doubt ("That's different from my commute")
= Hesitation before next leap
```

**Language for conflicted feelings:**
- "I'm... but also..."
- "It's good, yet..."
- "Part of me feels... another part..."
- "I like it, but there's also..."
- "Proud, but also questioning..."

### Diary vs. Conversation Speech Patterns

**Diary writing (Entry 018):**
```
- Fragments: "Clear skies."
- Rhetorical questions: "What's next?"
- Internal dialogue: "Do I have things like that too?"
- Present tense mixed with past
- Emotional immediacy
```

**Conversation (Minseo dialogue):**
```
- Questions: "How long are you gonna keep walking?"
- Simple responses: "I don't know"
- Acknowledging: "That's true."
- Suggestions: "Why not try running?"
- Casual comparisons: "you used to... you actually..."
```

### Emphasis Techniques

**Method 1: Repetition**
```
"Impossible. Absolutely impossible."
"So sweet." → "The fragrance is thick. So sweet."
"Walking has become routine. Normal."
```

**Method 2: Fragment placement**
```
"Running.
After lunch, I couldn't stop thinking about what he said."
(Isolated word for dramatic emphasis)
```

**Method 3: Rhetorical questions**
```
"Is that good or bad?"
"What's next?"
"Should I just keep walking like this?"
(All invite internal response, not external answer)
```

**Method 4: Casual negation**
```
"No particular thoughts."
"Without stopping to walk."
"Never noticed the citrus blossom scent."
```
