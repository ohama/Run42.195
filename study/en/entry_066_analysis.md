### Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| "I'm running TO school" (every time as destination) | "I'm running to school" OR "I'm doing a morning run" | Don't overuse "to" for destination; context clarifies |
| "Good job for keeping" | "You're keeping at it" | Use gerund (-ing) form; "keeping at it" is the right collocation |
| "It's a pity" | "That's too bad" | More natural in casual teen conversation |
| "Take photographs" | "Get some shots" / "Take pics" | More natural/casual than formal "photograph" |

## PART 4: PHRASAL VERBS (구동사)

### Phrasal Verb Analysis

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning (의미) | Original Meaning vs. Metaphorical | Example from Text | Alternative Forms |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **"get past"** | 통과하다, ~을 넘어서다 | Originally: physically pass through; Here: overcome/exceed distance | "Once I got past 11km, it got hard" | pass, surpass, exceed, push through |
| **"keep at it"** | 계속 노력하다, 꾸준히 하다 | Originally: remain in position; Here: sustained effort/perseverance | "You're keeping at it. Good job." | keep going, stick with it, persist, continue |
| **"turning [color]"** | (색으로) 변하다 | Originally: rotate; Here: seasonal color change | "The trees along the path were turning red" | change, shift, transform, become |
| **"run together"** | 함께 달리다 | Usually literal; Here: combined physical activity + companionship | "Wanna run together on Saturday?" | go for a run, do a run with, join for a run |
| **"report to"** | ~에게 보고하다 | Transfer information upward to authority | "I reported to my teacher" | tell, inform, notify, update |
| **"handle"** | (어려운 일을) 해내다, 처리하다 | Originally: physically grasp; Here: manage difficulty | "You'll be able to handle 17km next week too" | manage, deal with, accomplish, complete |

### Phrasal Verb Comparison: Similar Expressions

**Getting past/Overcoming Distance:**
- "get past 11km" (overcome at specific point)
- "push through the pain" (forceful perseverance)
- "make it to 15km" (arrival-focused)
- "finish strong at 17km" (emphasis on end)

**Persistence & Effort:**
- "keep at it" (sustained effort over time)
- "stick with it" (commitment/loyalty focus)
- "persist in running" (formal/determined)
- "keep going" (simpler, more urgent)

## PART 5: CONVERSATIONAL PATTERNS (회화 패턴)

### Pattern 1: "Wanna [Verb]?" - Casual Invitation

**Pattern Structure:**
```
Wanna [base verb] [location/time/details]?
= Do you want to / Would you like to
= Casual, friendly, non-committal tone
```

**Examples from text:**
- "Wanna run together on Saturday?" (토요일에 함께 달릴래?)

**Additional Examples:**
- "Wanna grab lunch?" (점심 먹고 싶어?)
- "Wanna see a movie tonight?" (오늘 밤 영화 볼래?)
- "Wanna come over later?" (나중에 놀러 올래?)

**When to use:** With friends, peers, people of equal status. NOT with teachers/elders.

**When NOT to use:** In formal settings, with authority figures.

**Alternatives:**
- More formal: "Do you want to run together on Saturday?"
- More polite: "Would you like to run together on Saturday?"
- More insistent: "Let's run together on Saturday!"

---

### Pattern 2: "How [adjective/far/much]?" - Direct Inquiry

**Pattern Structure:**
```
How [adjective]?
= Asking about quality/distance/quantity
= Brief, direct, assumes context
```

**Examples from text:**
- "How far?" (얼마나 멀리?)
- "How far?" (거리가 어떻게 돼?)

**Additional Examples:**
- "How far can you run?" (얼마나 멀리 뛸 수 있어?)
- "How long is the route?" (코스가 얼마나 길어?)
- "How many kilometers?" (몇 킬로미터예?)

**Context clues in response:**
Response should match the implied unit:
- Question: "How far?" → Answer: "7km" / "Maybe 10km?"
- Question: "How long?" → Answer: "About an hour" / "Maybe 1.5 hours"

---

### Pattern 3: "Yeah/Okay" - Minimal Agreement Response

**Pattern Structure:**
```
[Agreement word] [Optional: elaboration]
= Enthusiastic yes, eager agreement
```

**Examples from text:**
- "Yeah. I saw Hallasan this morning..." (네. 아침에 한라산을...)
- "Okay." (알겠어/좋아.)
- "Yeah. I heard the colors are incredible there." (응. 그곳 색깔이 정말 대단하다더라.)
- "Yeah, it was." (응, 그랬어.)

**Variations by enthusiasm:**
- "Yeah" = simple agreement
- "Sure" = willing agreement
- "Cool" = positive/impressed agreement
- "Okay" = accepting directive/information

---

### Pattern 4: "Once I [past action], it [changed state]" - Cause-Effect Narrative

**Pattern Structure:**
```
Once I [past action at marker], [consequence/change in state]
= Sequential narrative; threshold moment
= Shows progression through difficulty
```

**Examples from text:**
- "Once I got past 11km, it got hard" (11km을 넘으니까 힘들어졌어)

**Additional Examples:**
- "Once I started running, everything changed" (뛰기 시작하니까 모든 게 달라졌어)
- "Once we reached the mountains, the air felt different" (산에 도착하니까 공기가 달라 느껴졌어)
- "Once the sun went down, it got cold" (해가 지니까 추워졌어)

**Nuance:** Suggests a turning point; not just simple sequence but transformation.

---

### Pattern 5: "That makes sense" - Validating Response

**Pattern Structure:**
```
That makes sense.
= I understand the logic/reason
= Acknowledges validity of other's point
```

**Examples from text:**
- Teacher: "That makes sense. You haven't done that distance before." (말이 된다. 그 거리 처음 뛰는 거지.)

**Similar Expressions:**
- "Yeah, that's right" (그래, 맞아)
- "I get it" (이해해)
- "True" (맞다)
- "I see" (아, 알겠다)

**Conversational Function:** Shows active listening; validates the other person's experience/logic.

## PART 7: TEEN/CASUAL SPEECH FEATURES (구어체 특징)

### Incomplete Sentences & Fragmented Expression

| Feature | Korean Equivalent | Example from Text | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Single-word utterance** | 단어 하나만 사용 | "Beautiful." (아름다워) | Emotional exclamation; stands alone as complete thought |
| **Omitted subject** | 주어 생략 | "Running again today, huh?" (또 오늘도 뛰고 있네?) | Assumes context; familiar/casual |
| **Omitted verb** | 동사 생략 | "7km today. Running to school." | List-like narration; informal written style |
| **Time marker + state** | 시간 + 상태 | "Fall's gotten deeper already." | Direct observation; no elaborate structure |
| **"And [new thought]"** | "그리고 [새로운 생각]" | "And when winter passes, spring comes again." | Sequential addition; diary-like musing |

### Filler Words & Hedging Language

| Filler/Hedging Word | Korean Equivalent | Usage in Text | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"huh?"** | "네?" / "그쵤?" | "Running again today, huh?" | Seeks confirmation; assumes familiarity |
| **"maybe"** | "아마" | "Maybe 10km?" | Uncertainty; not final/firm |

| **"kind of"** | "뭔가" / "좀" | Not in this text, but would fit: "kind of tired" | Softens statement; reduces intensity |

## PART 8: SENTENCE STARTERS (문장 시작 표현)

### Honesty/Direct Statement Starters

| Starter | Function | Example | Korean Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"But seeing it while running"** | Contrasts expectation vs. reality | "But seeing it while running makes it feel more beautiful somehow" | "하지만 뛰면서 보니" |
| **"If I'd been on the bus"** | Counterfactual/what-if | "If I'd been on the bus, I would've just missed it" | "만약 버스를 탔었으면" |
| **"Once I got past 11km"** | Threshold/turning point | "Once I got past 11km, it got hard" | "11km을 넘으니까" |
| **"A year ago in spring"** | Temporal contrast | "A year ago in spring, I was getting carsick on the bus" | "일 년 전 봄에는" |

### Observation/Description Starters

| Starter | Function | Example | Korean Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"The [noun] was/were [adjective]"** | Direct observation | "The leaves are changing color" | "[명사]는 [형용사]였다" |
| **"[Noun] and [noun]"** | Pairing/juxtaposition | "Orange tangerines and red autumn leaves" | "[명사]와 [명사]" |
| **"This morning while running"** | Temporal + action frame | "This morning while running, the trees along the path were turning red" | "아침 뛰면서" |
| **"[Time marker], [observation]"** | Time-stamped note | "Fall's gotten deeper already" | "[시간], [관찰]" |

### Reflective/Philosophical Starters

| Starter | Function | Example | Korean Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"Seasons keep turning"** | Cyclical reflection | "Seasons keep turning. Now it's fall" | "계절이 계속 바뀌고" |
| **"Once [time period] passes, [consequence]"** | Future projection | "Once this season passes, winter will come" | "[시간]이 지나면 [결과]" |
| **"And when [condition], [result]"** | Continued reflection | "And when winter passes, spring comes again" | "그리고 [조건]이면 [결과]" |
| **"Now [contrast with past]"** | Personal transformation | "Now I'm running and seeing the autumn colors" | "이제 [현재 행동]" |
| **"I've [past action], [realization]"** | Retrospective insight | "I've changed a lot" | "많이 변했다" |
| **"Another [abstract noun]"** | Conclusion/meta-observation | "Another joy of running" | "[추상명사]의 또 다른 [것]" |

### Dialogue-Specific Starters

| Starter | Social Context | Example | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"Yeah" + elaboration** | Response to peer observation | "Yeah. I saw Hallasan this morning..." | Affirm + provide detail |
| **"Okay" (single word)** | Accepting directive | "Okay." (in response to "Next time, get some shots") | Quiet agreement |
| **"Let's [verb]"** | Proposal/inclusive plan | "Let's go see the leaves. Not the coastal road—toward the mountains." | Shared activity suggestion |
| **"Oh, [positive reaction]"** | Pleased response | "Oh, cool. Hallasan loop trail?" | Shows enthusiasm + checks understanding |

## FINAL SUMMARY

**Entry 066: The Leaves Are Changing** captures a pivotal moment in Doyun's journey where running has become not just a physical practice but a means of aesthetic appreciation and personal reflection. Key takeaways for English learners:

1. **Casual teen speech** uses fragments, hedging words, and minimal agreement ("Sure," "Cool")
2. **Seasonal vocabulary** ties to personal growth metaphors throughout this novel
3. **Phrasal verbs** like "keeping at it," "got past," "handle" are essential for speaking about endurance
4. **Conversational patterns** show respect hierarchies (formal with teacher, casual with friends, affectionate with family)
5. **Emotional expression** ranges from simple appreciation ("Beautiful") to profound realization ("I've changed a lot")

**Target Practice:** Focus on the dialogue patterns and casual speech markers—these are most authentic to how teen characters communicate in English fiction and are valuable for adult learners wanting natural speech patterns.
