### Semantic Categories of Phrasal Verbs


#### Temporal/Sleep
```

```

#### Mental/Emotional
```

```

### Extended Phrasal Verb Explanations

#### **"Go with you"**
- **Original meaning:** Travel in same direction
- **Text usage:** Family accompanying Doyun to race start
- **Nuance:** Implies solidarity, support, shared experience
- **Natural variations:** "come with you," "go along with you," "go together"
- **Example context:** "Dad: 'We'll go with you.' → Family is emotionally invested and physically present"

#### **"Get to bed early"**
- **Original meaning:** Achieve state of sleeping; reach the bed
- **Text usage:** Attempt to rest before stressful event
- **Nuance:** "Tried to" shows intention but implies difficulty/failure
- **Natural variations:** "get some sleep," "hit the bed," "get some rest"
- **Example context:** "Tried to get to bed early, but my heart was pounding" → showing sleeplessness despite intent

#### **"Close my eyes"**
- **Original meaning:** Shut eyelids physically
- **Text usage:** Accepting fate; ending the day mentally
- **Nuance:** Symbolic gesture of surrender and trust
- **Natural variations:** "shut my eyes," "close my eyes and accept it"
- **Example context:** "I closed my eyes. *Tomorrow. 42.195km.*" → literal action with metaphorical acceptance

### Comparison: Similar Phrasal Verbs with Different Meanings

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning | Difference |
|---|---|---|
| **go with** | Accompany | Active, together |
| **go alone** | Travel by oneself | Solo; independent |
| **come with** | Travel toward speaker | Direction toward speaker |
| **see you off** | Wave goodbye | Ceremonial departure |

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning | Difference |
|---|---|---|
| **get to bed** | Go to sleep | Achieving state of rest |
| **get into bed** | Physical position; lie down | Action of lying down |
| **stay in bed** | Remain sleeping | Continuing rest state |
| **get out of bed** | Rise from bed | Ending sleep state |

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning | Difference |
|---|---|---|
| **wait at** | Remain at location | Specific place |
| **wait for** | Anticipate arrival of | Specific person/thing |
| **wait up** | Remain awake | Time factor |
| **wait around** | Linger aimlessly | Casual, purposeless |

## PART 5: CONVERSATIONAL PATTERNS (회화 패턴)

Entry 103 demonstrates several important English conversational patterns used in pre-event communication and internal reflection.

### Pattern 1: Affirmation/Encouragement

**Structure:** `[Positive statement/imperative] + [Confident response]`

```
Friend: "Run well tomorrow!"
Response: "Thanks."

Friend: "You've got this."
Response: [Implied acceptance/nodding]

Pattern Formula:
─────────────────────────────────
[Supporter Statement]  →  [Brief Grateful Acknowledgment]
```

**Cultural Context for Korean Learners:**
- Short, clipped responses ("Thanks," "Cool") show confidence and maturity
- Not over-explaining shows self-assurance
- In Korean culture, longer gratitude is expected; English culture values brevity here

**5+ Example Dialogues:**

| Friend's Statement | Natural English Response | Korean Context |
|---|---|---|
| "Run well tomorrow!" | "Thanks." or "I will, thanks." | 응원에 대한 감사 |

| "Good luck!" | "Thanks, I've prepared for this." | 준비한 자신감 표현 |

### Contrast: Nervous vs. Scared (Crucial Distinction)

The text explicitly makes this distinction: **"I'm nervous. But I'm not scared."**

| State | Korean | Definition | Why Different |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nervous** (긴장) | Excited anticipation mixed with apprehension | Normal before important event; shows respect for challenge | Does not imply inability |
| **Scared** (무섭다) | Fear of failure; doubt in capability | Suggests lack of preparation; defeatist attitude | Implies possible negative outcome |

**Why this matters in English:**
- Korean often uses "scary" (무서워) for both nervousness and fear
- English distinguishes: "nervous" = normal excitement + slight anxiety
- English: "scared" = genuine dread + belief you might fail
- Doyun explicitly rejects "scared"—showing he trusts his preparation

**Example contrasts:**

### Category 2: Contrast/Shift Starters (대조 표현)

These introduce **contrasting** ideas or shifts in perspective:

| Starter | Korean | Effect | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **But** | 하지만 | Negation of previous statement | "I'm nervous. But I'm not scared." |

| **Yet** | 아직 | Anticipation; not-yet quality | [Not explicitly used, but implied] |

**Contrast Pattern in Entry:**
```
PAST STATE              →    CONTRAST    →    PRESENT STATE
Bus rides felt sick     →    But now     →    About to run 42km
Walking 7km impossible  →    versus      →    Running marathon
Dreading movement       →    yet         →    Prepared and focused
```

**How to Use Contrast Starters:**

1. **"But"** - Show unexpected twist
   - "I was tired. But I kept going."
   - "Seemed impossible. But I did it."

2. **"Just"** - Emphasize simplicity
   - "Just took one more step."
   - "Just keep focusing."

3. **"Now"** - Emphasize present vs. past
   - "Now I'm ready."
   - "Now I understand why."

### Dialogue Practice #1: Pre-Race Text Exchange

**Scenario:** Doyun receives encouragement the night before the marathon. This simulates real text communication patterns.

```
MINSEO:
"Hey! How are you feeling about tomorrow?"

DOYUN:
"Nervous, honestly. But I've prepared well."

MINSEO:
```

**Key Language Points:**
- Short, punchy exchanges (text style)
- Emotional honesty ("Nervous, honestly")
- Gratitude ("That really means a lot")
- Humility with confidence ("I'm ready")
- Specific support commitment ("at the 20km mark")

### Dialogue Practice #2: Family Conversation at Dinner

**Scenario:** Evening before race; family discussing plans.

```
DOYUN:
"Mom, Dad, I wanted to talk about tomorrow morning."

DAD:
```

**Key Language Points:**
- Questions for information ("What time do you need to leave?")
- Statements of commitment ("We'll drive you")
- Specific details (times, distances)
- Emotional support ("We believe in you")
- Gratitude ("I'm grateful")

### Dialogue Practice #3: Peer Encouragement at School

**Scenario:** Friend group discussing Doyun's upcoming marathon at school.

```
FRIEND 1:
```

**Key Language Points:**
- Acknowledgment of past limitations ("barely walk")

- Realistic assessment ("won't know till I try")
- Peer validation ("you should be proud")
- Emotional appreciation ("means a lot")

### Dialogue Practice #4: Self-Affirmation/Mental Preparation

**Scenario:** Doyun alone in room, speaking his thoughts aloud before sleep.

```
DOYUN (to himself):

"I can do this."
```

**Key Language Points:**
- Specific goal restatement ("42.195 kilometers")
- Contrasting past/present ("couldn't... now I'm...")
- Self-realization ("That's amazing")
- Emotional honesty ("I'm nervous")
- Evidence-based confidence ("I've prepared," "I've trained")
- Positive visualization ("smiling at finish")
- Final affirmation ("I can do this")
