Essential expressions for daily interactions, greetings, and casual conversation about ordinary activities.

## Practice Dialogue 1: At the Market

**Scenario:** Elderly person offers fruit to a young person

```
Person A (Elder): "Here, take these with you. They're fresh today."
Person B (Young): "What? This many? I couldn't possibly—"
Person A: "Just a few. Nothing much. Do you like fruit?"
Person B: "Yes, I do, but... this is too kind of you."
Person A: "Don't be silly. It's just a handful of tangerines."
Person B: "Well... thank you so much. I really appreciate it."
```

## Practice Dialogue 2: About Daily Work

```
Person A: "You come out here every day? Doesn't it get tiring?"
Person B: "Sure it does. But I've been doing this for so long, it's just routine now."
Person A: "What keeps you going?"
Person B: "I don't know, really. It's just what I do. But honestly, I don't mind it anymore."
Person A: "That's nice. Having work you can accept."
```

## Practice Dialogue 3: Sharing Food

```
Person A: "Hey, eat these."
Person B: "What? Where'd you get them?"
Person A: "Someone gave them to me. Thought I'd share."
Person B: "Oh man, these are good! Nothing beats fresh fruit."
Person A: "Right? That's why they're special—they're genuine."
```

## Common Mistakes

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| "Here, take these to you" | "Here, take these with you" | "with you" is idiomatically correct for carrying something away |
| "How many? This much?" | "What? This many?" | Use "What?" as surprised reaction, not "How many?" |
| "He gave me a lot of tangerines" | "He handed me a whole bag" | More vivid and specific; shows the act and volume |
| "I started to like walking" | "I came to like walking" / "I started liking walking" | Both correct; second is more natural in narrative |
| "Don't need reason" | "Don't need a reason" | Article "a" is required here |

# PART 4: PHRASAL VERBS (구동사)

Multi-word verbs combining a verb with prepositions or adverbs, creating new meanings.

## Phrasal Verbs from Entry 012

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning | Korean | Example from Text | Similar Verbs |
|--------------|---------|--------|-------------------|---------------|
| **putter around** | Spend time doing small, aimless tasks; work leisurely | "이리저리 돌아다니며 일하다" | "Come out in the morning, putter around, and suddenly it's lunchtime." | tinker around / fiddle around / potter about |
| **pass by** | Go near something; not stop | "지나가다" | "When I walk to school, I pass by a tangerine orchard." | go by / walk past / come by |

| **hand over** | Give something directly to someone | "건네주다" | "he handed me a whole bag" | pass / give / deliver |
| **open up** / **open the bag** | Unfold or access something | "열다" | "When I got to school, I opened the bag." | unfold / unlock / reveal |
| **peel** (+ object) | Remove outer layer | "벗기다, 껍질을 까다" | "Minseo peeled one as he talked." | strip / shed / remove skin |

## Phrasal Verb Structure Analysis

### Pattern 1: Verb + Adverb Particle
```
putter + AROUND = spend time doing small tasks
flash + BY = move quickly past
peel + (away) = remove outer layer

Usage: Intransitive (no object) or Transitive (with object)
"He puttered around." (intransitive)
"I peeled one." (transitive)
```

### Pattern 2: Verb + Preposition
```
pass + BY = go near something without stopping
hand + OVER = give to someone

Position: Preposition often follows the object
"He handed me a bag." (object goes between verb and preposition)
"He handed the bag to me." (alternative structure)
```

## Similar Verbs Comparison

**"Give" family - Different ways to transfer objects:**

| Expression | Nuance | Formality | Context |
|-----------|--------|-----------|---------|
| **give** | Most common, neutral | All levels | "Give me the apple" |
| **hand (something to someone)** | Physical, direct contact | Casual | "He handed me the bag" |
| **pass** | Implies movement across space | Casual | "Pass the salt, please" |
| **hand over** | Implies surrender or formality | More formal | "Hand over your wallet" |
| **deliver** | Official, intentional | Formal | "The package was delivered" |

**In Entry 012:** "he handed me a whole bag" is warm, personal, and shows the direct human connection.

# PART 5: CONVERSATIONAL PATTERNS (회화 패턴)

Recurring structures and formulas for speaking in natural, authentic English dialogue.

## Pattern 1: Expressing Surprise with "What?"

**Structure:** "What?" + statement

```
"What? This many?"
"What? Tangerines?"
"Dude, you guys are really close now."
"What? [Statement from other person]"
```

**Nuance:** The question word "What?" acts as a reaction marker, not a real question.
- Signals surprise or disbelief
- Often followed by short, declarative statement
- Very natural in English conversation
- More casual than "Really?" or "You're kidding?"

**Alternative patterns:**
- "What? Seriously?" (adds doubt)
- "What, you're giving me tangerines?" (adds accusation/disbelief)
- "What do you mean?" (asks for clarification)

**Practice dialogue:**
```
A: "I got tangerines from someone I just met."
B: "What? From a stranger?"
A: "Well, not exactly a stranger. Someone I say hi to every morning."
B: "What? Every single morning?"
A: "Yeah. For a while now."
```

## Pattern 2: Seeking Clarification & Understanding

**Structure:** Short question + hesitation + follow-up

```
"Why do you walk, anyway?"
"Huh?"
"Wouldn't the bus be easier? Why bother walking?"
```

**Flow:**
1. Initial question (sets topic)
2. "Huh?" (listener didn't hear, or is confused)
3. Rephrasing/elaboration (asks same question differently)

**Why this pattern works:**
- Shows natural, unscripted conversation
- Allows time for thinking
- Provides multiple chances to understand
- Feels authentic (people don't always catch everything)

**Similar exchanges:**
```
A: "Do you ever get tired of this routine?"
B: "Huh?"
A: "I mean, doing the same thing every day. Doesn't that exhaust you?"
B: "Oh, sure it does. But I'm used to it."
```

## Pattern 3: Answering with Uncertainty

**Structure:** "I [verb]. Probably not." / "I don't know. But..."

```
"Have I ever done something like that for someone?"
"I don't know. Probably not."
```

**This pattern expresses:**
- Self-reflection
- Honest uncertainty
- Acceptance without judgment
- Openness to change

**Variations:**
- "I don't really know why." (honest admission)
- "I don't know. But next time..." (uncertainty + future action)
- "I'm not sure. Maybe..." (tentative speculation)

**In natural conversation:**
```
A: "Have you ever thought about what you want to do?"
B: "I don't know. I've thought about it, but not seriously."
A: "What appeals to you?"
B: "I'm not sure. Maybe something outdoors?"
```

## Pattern 4: Adding New Information with "But"

**Structure:** Statement + "But" + contrasting or adding detail

```
"I get motion sickness on the bus. That's why I started walking."
"But it's not just about the motion sickness anymore."
```

**This pattern:**
- Introduces a complication or development
- Suggests growth or change
- Provides new insight
- Contradicts or expands previous statement

**Examples from text:**
- "It felt good, but it was confusing."
- "I thanked him, but I didn't know what to say."
- "I only know him a little, but I want to do something for him."

## Pattern 5: Making Philosophical Observations

**Structure:** Observation + generalization + conclusion

```
"Maybe he just wanted to give them. That's it."
"That's probably it. Kindness without reason. For the grandfather, that must just be natural."
```

**Components:**
1. **Initial thought:** "Maybe he just wanted to give them"
2. **Confirmation:** "That's probably it"
3. **Principle:** "Kindness without reason"
4. **Generalization:** "that must just be natural"

**Similar patterns:**
- "That's how it goes. That's a good thing."
- "Don't need a reason for something good to be good."

# PART 10: EXTENDED PRACTICE (연습)

Comprehensive practice activities combining multiple language skills.

## Dialogue Practice Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Talking with a Friend About an Unexpected Gift

**Context:** You received something nice from someone you barely know. Your friend is curious.

```
FRIEND: "Hey, what's that you're eating?"
YOU: "Oh, these? Someone gave them to me."
FRIEND: "What? From who?"
YOU: "There's this grandfather I see every morning when I walk to school. He works at a tangerine orchard."
FRIEND: "Seriously? You just say hi, and he's giving you fruit?"
YOU: "Yeah, I was surprised too. He said to share them with friends."
FRIEND: "Dude, you guys are really close now."
YOU: "I don't know if I'd say close, but... I feel like I should do something for him."
FRIEND: "Like what?"
YOU: "I'm not sure yet. Maybe just keep greeting him well every morning."
```

**Key expressions used:**
- Expressing surprise: "What? From who?"
- Explaining circumstances: "Someone gave them to me"
- Describing relationship: "I just say hi"
- Expressing intention: "I feel like I should do something"
- Showing uncertainty: "I'm not sure yet"

**Your turn:** Practice this dialogue with a partner, using your own gift/experience.

### Scenario 2: Conversation with a Parent About Daily Kindness

**Context:** You're explaining to your parent why someone was kind to you.

```
PARENT: "Where did you get these tangerines?"
YOU: "A grandfather at the orchard gave them to me."
PARENT: "That was kind of him. Why did he do that?"
YOU: "I'm not exactly sure. I say hi every morning when I walk past, and today he just... handed me a whole bag."
PARENT: "That's very thoughtful. You should show him respect."
YOU: "I know. I want to do something for him, but I don't know what he needs."
PARENT: "The best thing you can do is greet him well. Show genuine kindness in return."
YOU: "Yeah, that's what I'm planning to do. Just a cheerful 'good morning' every day."
PARENT: "That's perfect. Sometimes kindness is just acknowledgment."
```

**Language points:**
- Describing someone's action: "he just handed me a whole bag"
- Expressing gratitude: "That was kind of him"
- Showing respect: "I want to show him respect"
- Reciprocal action: "do something for him"
- Simple kindness: "Just a cheerful greeting"

**Role play:** Switch roles with a partner. Practice as both parent and child.

### Scenario 3: Reflecting with a Friend About What Kindness Means

**Context:** Deep conversation about why people do kind things.

```
FRIEND A: "So he just gave you fruit? No reason?"
FRIEND B: "That's what I couldn't understand at first. But then I thought... maybe he doesn't need a reason."
FRIEND A: "What do you mean?"
FRIEND B: "Well, I think for him, kindness is just natural. He doesn't do it expecting something in return."
FRIEND A: "That's pretty rare, isn't it?"
FRIEND B: "Yeah. And it made me realize I should be more like that. Just give sometimes, without waiting for a reason to be perfect."
FRIEND A: "Have you thought about what you want to do for him?"
FRIEND B: "Not yet. But I'll figure something out. Maybe just being there, greeting him well every morning."
FRIEND A: "That matters more than you might think."
FRIEND B: "You know, you're right. That probably means something to him."
```

**Language points:**
- Philosophical statement: "Kindness doesn't need a reason"
- Personal realization: "I realized I should..."
- Expressing admiration: "That's pretty rare"
- Making commitments: "I'll figure something out"
- Accepting wisdom: "You're right"

**Discussion:** What does this conversation reveal about the characters' values?
