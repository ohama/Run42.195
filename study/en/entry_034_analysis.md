## Usage Notes

### Formality Spectrum
- **Most Formal:** "Clear skies," "Warning signs," "Heatstroke is dangerous"
- **Neutral:** "Heat wave," "At dawn," "Definitely different"
- **Most Casual:** "Must be," "That's rough," "Whoa," "My back was soaked"

### Variations & Alternatives

| Original | Similar Expression | Usage Context |
|---|---|---|
| "Heat wave" | "Extreme heat," "Scorching heat," "Heatwave conditions" | Weather reporting; formal discussion |
| "Must be the humidity" | "It's probably the humidity," "I think it's the humidity," "Could be the humidity" | Informal speculation; less certain |
| "Completely drained" | "Exhausted," "Wiped out," "Beat," "Shattered" | Casual; intensity increases left to right |
| "Good call" | "Good decision," "Smart move," "That was wise," "Right choice" | Informal approval; getting more formal right |
| "Don't overdo it" | "Don't push yourself," "Take it easy," "Don't go too hard" | Advice; varying intensity |
| "Whoa" | "Wow," "Geez," "Yikes," "Oh wow" | Surprise; different emotional tone |

## Practice Dialogue 1: Discussing Summer Heat

**Scenario:** Two colleagues at work discussing summer running

```
A: "I tried running this morning, but it was already 27 degrees at 5:30."
B: "That's brutal. Did you finish your usual distance?"
A: "No, I had to stop at 3km. My head was spinning."
B: "Good call stopping when you did. Heatstroke is dangerous."
A: "Yeah, the nurse told me to drink plenty of water. I definitely will."
B: "Smart. Maybe run in the evening when it cools down?"
A: "That's the plan. My friend and I are running at 7 tomorrow evening."
```

## Practice Dialogue 2: Giving Health Advice

**Scenario:** Parent checking on a teenager returning from exercise

```
Parent: "Your face is all red! What happened?"
Teen: "I was running, but it's way too hot outside. I got dizzy."
Parent: "You should rest up. Drink plenty of water."
Teen: "I know. I already drank my whole bottle."
Parent: "That's good. Here, eat some watermelon. You need to hydrate."

```

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct Form | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| "My back was completely wet" | "My back was soaked/drenched" | "Soaked" implies thorough saturation; "wet" is too mild |
| "The moment I starting running" | "The moment I started running" | Grammar: past tense required |
| "It must is the humidity" | "It must be the humidity" | Cannot combine "must" + "is"; use "must be" |
| "Stop at 3km" (as present) | "I had to stop at 3km" | Context requires completed action; past tense needed |
| "Very hot" (repeated) | "Scorching," "blazing," "brutal," "sweltering" | Variety prevents repetition; stronger adjectives for intensity |

The entry effectively builds physical sensation from minor to severe:

1. **Minor discomfort:** "Breathing felt heavier"
2. **Moderate distress:** "Air was sticky," "Back was soaked"

## Common Sensory Expression Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct | Why |

| "I'm very hot" (repetitive) | "I'm sweltering," "I'm roasting," "I'm burning up" | More specific; avoids vague "very" |

# PART 4: PHRASAL VERBS (구동사)

## Phrasal Verbs in Entry 034

### Phrasal Verbs from the Text

| Phrasal Verb | Base Meaning | Textual Meaning | Example from Entry |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"stop at [distance]"** | cease movement | interrupt run at specific point | "I had to stop at 3km" |
| **"push myself"** (+ too hard) | exert effort | exceed safe limits | "If I'd pushed myself, I could've gotten heatstroke" |
| **"run in [weather]"** | exercise during | attempt during conditions | "You ran in this weather?" |
| **"catch my breath"** | regain breathing | recover from exertion | "I walked. Caught my breath." |
| **"drink up"** (implicit) | consume liquid | finish water supply | "My bottle emptied fast" |
| **"look up"** | research; search | investigate information | "I'd looked up before... Tips for running in summer" |
| **"slump onto"** | collapse heavily | sit exhaustedly | "I slumped onto a bench" |
| **"stick to"** | maintain; adhere | stay within area | "I stuck to the shade" |

### Similar Verbs Comparison

#### "Stop" vs. "Stop at" vs. "Stop by" vs. "Cut short"

| Verb | Korean | Usage | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stop** | 멈추다 | Complete cessation | "I had to stop running." |
| **Stop at [distance]** | [거리]에서 멈추다 | Cease at specific point | "I had to stop at 3km." |
| **Stop by** | 들르다 | Brief visit/detour | "I stopped by the nurse's office." |
| **Cut short** | 중단하다 | Premature termination | "I cut short my run because of heat." |

#### "Push (myself)" vs. "Push through" vs. "Overdo it"

| Expression | Korean | Intensity | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Push myself** | 열심히 하다 | Effort; neutral | "I push myself every day." |
| **Push through** | 밀어붙이다 | Persistence despite difficulty | "I pushed through the pain." |
| **Overdo it** | 무리하다 | Exceeding safe limits ★★★★★ | "Don't overdo it in this heat." |
| **Go too hard** | 너무 열심히 하다 | Excessive intensity | "If I'd gone too hard, I'd get heatstroke." |

#### "Stick to" vs. "Stay in" vs. "Keep to"

| Expression | Korean | Precision | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stick to [area]** | [장소]를 지키다 | Actively maintain location | "I stuck to the shade." |
| **Stay in [area]** | [장소]에 있다 | Remain in location | "Stay in the shade." |
| **Keep to [path]** | [길]을 지키다 | Follow route; more formal | "Keep to the shaded path." |

## Phrasal Verb Patterns: Heat/Exercise Context

### Pattern 1: STOP + LOCATION/DISTANCE
```
Structure: "stop at [distance/place]"

Examples:
- I had to stop at 3km. (멈춘 지점)
- We stopped at the park. (멈춘 장소)
- She stops at 5km on hot days. (일반적 습관)
```

### Pattern 2: PUSH + INTENSITY
```
Structure: "push myself + adverb" or "push [too] hard"

Examples:
- Don't push yourself in weather like this. (과도하게 노력하지 마)
- If I'd pushed myself, I could've gotten heatstroke. (더 열심히 했으면)
- He pushes too hard during summer. (강도가 높다)
```

### Pattern 3: CATCH + RECOVERY
```
Structure: "catch one's breath" (호흡을 회복하다)

Examples:
- I walked. Caught my breath. (숨을 고르다)
- She needed to catch her breath. (숨을 쉴 필요가 있었다)
- Let me catch my breath for a second. (잠깐 쉬게 해)
```

# PART 5: CONVERSATIONAL PATTERNS (회화 패턴)

## Conversational Patterns in Entry 034

### Pattern 1: STATING LIMITATIONS / ADMITTING STRUGGLE

**Formula:** "I could only [action] [limitation]"

```
Korean: "나는 [행동]만 할 수 있었다 [제약]"
Pattern: "I could only [verb] [how far/how long/how much]"

Examples from entry:
- "I could only run 3km today. Too hot." (제한된 범위를 인정)
- "Dude, I could only run 3km today." (친구에게 불가능함 설명)
```

**Usage:** Admitting limited performance; explaining constraints to others

**Tone:** Resigned; matter-of-fact; sometimes frustrated

**Related Patterns:**
- "I was only able to..." (더 공식적)
- "I managed to..." (긍정적 강조)
- "I had to stop at..." (강제된 중단)

**Practice:**
- "I could only stay for 30 minutes."
- "I could only eat half the plate."
- "I could only run 2km before my legs gave out."

### Pattern 2: STATING CONDITION / WARNING

**Formula:** "[Phenomenon] is [Adjective]"

```
Korean: "[현상]은 [형용사]이다"
Pattern: "[Topic] is [dangerous/serious/important/critical]"

Examples from entry:
- "Heatstroke is dangerous." (경고; 일반적 사실)
- "Running at midday is absolutely dangerous." (확대된 경고)
- "Weather like this is rough." (동의 구하기)
```

**Usage:** Making statements of fact; issuing warnings; emphasizing danger

**Tone:** Serious; cautionary; sometimes conversational

**Related Patterns:**
- "You can't [action] in [condition]." (방지하기)
- "It's too [adjective] for [action]." (부정/피하기)
- "Be careful about [noun]." (경고하기)

**Practice:**
- "Running in midday heat is dangerous."
- "Dehydration is serious."
- "This weather is brutal for exercise."

### Pattern 3: SEEKING CONFIRMATION / CHECKING UNDERSTANDING

**Formula:** "So [restatement of plan/idea]?" or "[Statement], right?"

```
Korean: "그래서 [재진술]?" 또는 "[진술], 맞지?"
Pattern: "[Summarize] + question tag" or "So [restate]?"

Examples from entry:
- "So can we still run on Saturday?" (계획이 여전히 가능한지 확인)
- "Then evening. After sunset." (대안 제안)
- "How about 7?" (시간 제안; 확인 구함)
- "Saturday. The day we planned to run together." (상기; 확인)
```

**Usage:** Clarifying plans; checking if ideas work; confirming details

**Tone:** Collaborative; seeking agreement; planning-focused

**Related Patterns:**
- "That sounds good, right?"
- "Does that work?"
- "Can we still...?"
- "What about [alternative]?"

**Practice:**
- "So we're meeting at 7, then?"
- "The evening run works better for you?"
- "Can we do Saturday instead?"
- "What about early morning—does that work?"

### Pattern 4: RESPONDING TO ADVICE / ACCEPTING WARNING

**Formula:** "Yeah..." or "[Action], right?" (showing understanding)

```
Korean: "응..." 또는 "[행동], 맞지?" (이해 표시)
Pattern: "[Acknowledgment] + [restatement]" or short affirmative

Examples from entry:
- "Yeah..." (단순 인정; 더 이상 반박 안 함)
- "Yeah. But I stopped at 3km." (인정하면서 스스로 조치했음 설명)
- "Yeah. It was 27 degrees at dawn." (동의하면서 증거 제시)
- "Yeah. But I don't want to stop running." (인정하면서 반발도 표현)
```

**Usage:** Acknowledging advice; showing understanding; accepting caution

**Tone:** Compliant; thoughtful; sometimes reluctant

**Related Patterns:**
- "I know, but..." (인정하면서 반박)
- "Yeah, you're right..." (전적 동의)
- "I understand, but..." (이해하면서 다른 의견)

**Practice:**
- "Yeah, I get it. No running at midday."
- "I know. That's why I stopped."
