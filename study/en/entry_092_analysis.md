## PART 4: PHRASAL VERBS (구동사)

### 4.1 Phrasal Verbs in Entry 092

| Phrasal Verb | Korean | Meaning | Usage | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **pass by** | "지나가다" / "옆을 지나가다" | Go past or beyond | Physical movement during run | "I passed by Grandfather's field" |
| **give away** | "나눠주다" | Distribute/provide | Grandfather's action with tangerines | (Not directly used, but "gave me" = similar) |
| **back in** | "(다시) 돌아오다" | Return to a place | Temporal/spatial movement | "I'm back in my room" |
| **gone away** | "사라지다" / "없어지다" | Disappear/diminish | Physical sensation of soreness | "soreness has gone away" |

### 4.2 Detailed Analysis: Key Phrasal Verbs

#### PASSED BY (지나갔어)

**Structure:**
- Base: "pass" (지나가다)
- Particle: "by" (~를 지나)
- Full verb: "pass by" = 옆을 지나가다

**Original meaning:** To go past something
**Figurative meaning:** To let an opportunity pass

**Example from text:**
- "I passed by Grandfather's field." = 할아버지 밭을 지나갔어

**Comparison with similar verbs:**
- "Walk past" - slower, deliberate movement
- "Run by" - same action but emphasizes the sport
- "Pass" - simpler, less specific
- **In this context:** "passed by" is perfect for a runner's journey

**Usage variations:**
- "I passed by your house yesterday" (친구의 집을 지나갔어)
- "Don't pass by this opportunity" (이 기회를 놓치지 마)
- "She passed by without saying hello" (인사도 안 하고 지나갔어)

#### BACK IN (방에 돌아와서)

**Structure:**
- "back" (되돌아)
- "in" (안으로/안에)
- **Full phrase:** back in = ~로 돌아왔다, 다시 ~으로

**Original meaning:** Return to a location
**In diary context:** Marking the shift from external action to internal reflection

**Example from text:**
- "I'm back in my room writing this" = 방에 돌아와서 이렇게 쓰고 있어

**Similar expressions:**
- "I'm back home" = "집으로 돌아왔어"
- "I'm in my room" = "나는 방에 있어" (static)
- **Difference:** "back in" suggests a return; "in" is just location

**Usage variations:**
- "Back in school, I never thought about running" (학교 있을 때)
- "Are you back in training?" (훈련을 다시 시작했어?)
- "I'm back in the game" (다시 경기에 참여했어)

#### GONE AWAY (사라졌어)

**Structure:**
- "gone" (past participle of "go")
- "away" (멀리, 없어져)
- **Full phrase:** gone away = 없어졌다, 사라졌다

**Original meaning:** Depart/disappear
**In physical context:** Soreness diminishing with recovery

**Example from text:**
- "The soreness has mostly gone away" = 근육통이 대부분 사라졌어

**Similar expressions:**
- "The pain has gone away" = "통증이 없어졌어"
- "My headache has gone" = "두통이 없어졌어" (simpler)
- **Subtle difference:** "gone away" emphasizes a process; "gone" is instant

**Spectrum of disappearance:**
- Pain is gone away ☆☆☆☆☆ (完全 완화)
- Mostly gone away ★☆☆☆☆ (부분적 개선)
- Hasn't gone away ★★★★★ (여전함)

### 4.7 Cultural Context: Phrasal Verbs in English

Korean students often find phrasal verbs challenging because Korean typically uses single verbs with particles. Understanding that English combines verbs + prepositions for subtle meaning shifts helps:

- Korean: "지나가다" (single verb)
- English: "pass" (verb) + "by" (preposition) = "pass by" (more visual/descriptive)

This is natural for native English speakers but requires conscious learning for Korean speakers.

## PART 5: CONVERSATIONAL PATTERNS (회화 패턴)

### 5.1 Pattern: Confirming/Acknowledging Time or Situation

**Formula:**
```
"Oh, right. [Situation], isn't it. [Response/acknowledgment]."
```

**Korean Equivalent:**
```
"그러네, [상황]이네. [반응]."
```

**Examples from text:**
- "Oh, right. It is New Year's, isn't it. You too." (할아버지의 반응)

**Related structures:**
- "Yeah, right. It's Friday, isn't it." = 맞다, 금요일이네.
- "That's true. It's getting cold, isn't it." = 그래, 추워지고 있네.

**Variations in formality:**
| Formal | ★★★☆☆ | "Oh, that's right. It is New Year's, isn't it?" |
| Casual | ★☆☆☆☆ | "Oh yeah, it's New Year's, right?" |
| Very casual | ★☆☆☆☆ | "Oh right, New Year's! You too!" |

**Practice patterns:**
1. "Oh, right. It's already March, isn't it."
2. "Yeah, right. You're in high school now, aren't you."
3. "That's true. We're almost at the finish line, aren't we."

### 5.2 Pattern: Mother's Observation / Affectionate Statement

**Formula:**
```
"[Mom/Parent said], '[Observation about personal growth/change].'"
```

**Korean Equivalent:**
```
"엄마가 말했어, '[성장/변화에 대한 관찰].'"
```

**Examples from text:**
- "You're a year older now," Mom said.

**This pattern emphasizes:**
- Family context
- Growth/aging acknowledgment
- Warm, parental perspective

**Variations:**
- "You're taller now," Mom said.
- "You're stronger than last year," Mom noted.
- "Look how much you've changed," Dad observed.
- "You used to be so tired," Mom remembered.

**Practice dialogue:**
```
Mom: You're a year older now.
Doyun: I know, Mom.
Mom: Last year, you could barely run 5km.
Doyun: I know. Now I'm training for a marathon.
Mom: You've changed so much.
Doyun: Yeah, I guess I have.
```

### 5.3 Pattern: Reflection on Personal Change

**Formula:**
```
"[Past situation]. [Current situation]. [Summary of change]."
```

**Korean Equivalent:**
```
"[지난 상황]. [현재 상황]. [변화에 대한 요약]."
```

**Example from text:**
```
"Last year on this day, I was worried about bus sickness.
This year, I'm training for a full marathon.
Things have changed a lot."
```

**This pattern emphasizes:**
- Before/after contrast
- Personal growth
- Awareness of passage of time
- Gratitude/wonder at progress

**Practice variations:**
1. "Last month, I could only do 10km. Now I just finished 30km. I'm getting stronger."
2. "A year ago, I hated running. Today, I love it. Life is weird, right?"
3. "Last year, I dreaded the bus ride. This year, I run marathons. Everything's different."

**Expanded practice dialogue:**

```

Doyun: Really? In what way?
Friend: You used to hate bus rides, remember?
Doyun: Yeah... I'd get sick.
Friend: Now you're training for a marathon!

Friend: And now?
Doyun: Now I can do 30km. 38 days until the real race.

```
