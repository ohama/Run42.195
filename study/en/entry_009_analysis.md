# PART 1: EVERYDAY EXPRESSIONS (일상 표현)

## 1.1 Family Communication Expressions

### "I barely talked to Mom over the weekend."

**Original Korean:** "주말 내내 엄마와 거의 대화를 안 했다."

**Analysis:**
- "barely" = 거의 ~않다 (부정의 뉘앙스)
- 어색한 가족 관계를 표현

**"Barely" Usage Patterns:**

| Expression | Meaning | Nuance |
|------------|---------|--------|
| "I barely talked to her." | 거의 말 안 했다 | 어색함/갈등 |
| "I barely slept." | 거의 못 잤다 | 피곤함 |
| "I barely ate." | 거의 못 먹었다 | 식욕 없음/바쁨 |
| "I barely made it." | 겨우 해냈다/도착했다 | 아슬아슬 |
| "I barely remember." | 거의 기억 안 난다 | 희미한 기억 |

**Variations by Intensity:**

| Less | Standard | More |
|------|----------|------|
| "I didn't talk much." | "I barely talked." | "I didn't say a word." |
| "We hardly spoke." | "I barely talked." | "We were completely silent." |

**Practice Dialogue:**
```
A: How was your weekend?
B: Awkward, honestly. I barely talked to my mom.
A: Why? Did something happen?
B: We had a disagreement on Thursday.
A: That's tough. Are things better now?
B: Yeah, she spoke first on Sunday. We worked it out.
```

**Common Mistakes:**
- ❌ "I barely not talked to Mom." (이중 부정 불필요)
- ✅ "I barely talked to Mom."
- ❌ "I barely talked with Mom." (talk to가 더 자연스러움)
- ✅ "I barely talked to Mom."

### "Mom spoke first."

**Original Korean:** "엄마가 먼저 말을 꺼냈다."

**Analysis:** 화해의 첫 걸음을 표현

**Who Speaks First - Expressions:**

| English | Korean | Context |
|---------|--------|---------|
| "She spoke first." | 먼저 말했다 | 중립적 |
| "She broke the silence." | 침묵을 깼다 | 긴 침묵 후 |
| "She made the first move." | 먼저 다가왔다 | 화해 시도 |
| "She reached out." | 먼저 연락했다 | 관계 회복 |


### "Okay. But promise me."

**Original Korean:** "알겠어. 대신 약속해."

**Grammar Point:** Conditional permission with "But"

**Structure:**
```
[Permission] + But + [Condition/Promise]
```

**More Examples:**

| Permission | Condition | Full Sentence |
|------------|-----------|---------------|
| Okay | promise me | "Okay. But promise me you'll be careful." |
| Fine | just this once | "Fine. But just this once." |
| Alright | don't stay out too late | "Alright. But don't stay out too late." |
| Sure | call me when you get there | "Sure. But call me when you get there." |

**Parent-Child Negotiation Phrases:**

| Parent Says | Child Responds |
|-------------|----------------|
| "Promise me." | "I promise." |
| "Do you understand?" | "Yes, I understand." |
| "Can I trust you?" | "You can trust me." |
| "Don't make me regret this." | "I won't." |

### "Got it. I promise."

**Original Korean:** "알겠어요. 약속할게요."

**"Got it" Analysis:**
- 이해했다는 간결한 표현
- casual하지만 무례하지 않음
- 부모님께도 사용 가능 (한국어의 "알겠어요"처럼)

**Variations of "I understand":**

| Expression | Formality | Nuance |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| "Got it." | Casual | 간결, 확실한 이해 |
| "I got it." | Casual | 위와 동일 |
| "Okay, I understand." | Neutral | 표준 |
| "I see." | Neutral | 이해했다 (동의는 아닐 수 있음) |
| "Understood." | Formal | 군대/직장 |
| "I hear you." | Neutral | 네 말 들었다 |

## 1.2 Promise & Condition Expressions

### "Eat breakfast before you go."

**Original Korean:** "아침 먹고 가."

**Grammar Point:** Imperative with time clause

**Structure:**
```
[동사원형] + before/after + you + [동사]
```

**Mom's Safety Instructions:**

| English | Korean | Category |
|---------|--------|----------|
| "Eat breakfast before you go." | 밥 먹고 가 | 건강 |
| "Always stop at crosswalks." | 항상 횡단보도에서 멈춰 | 안전 |
| "Look both ways." | 좌우 살펴 | 안전 |
| "Don't walk with earphones in." | 이어폰 끼고 걷지 마 | 안전 |
| "If it gets too hard, catch a bus." | 힘들면 버스 타 | 건강 |

**Practice Dialogue:**
```
A: I'm heading out!
B: Wait! Did you eat breakfast?
A: I'm not hungry...
B: Eat something before you go. Even just toast.
A: Fine, fine. I'll grab something.
B: And remember—look both ways at crosswalks!
A: I know, Mom. I'm not a little kid.
B: You're always my kid. Be safe.
```

### "And twice a week max."

**Original Korean:** "그리고 일주일에 두 번만."

**"Max" Usage:**
- "maximum"의 축약형
- casual한 제한 표현

**Setting Limits - Expressions:**

| Expression | Meaning | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| "[number] max" | 최대 ~개 | "Two times a week max." |
| "No more than" | ~이하 | "No more than twice a week." |
| "At most" | 기껏해야 | "At most, three times." |
| "Tops" | 최대한 | "Three days, tops." |
| "Limit it to" | ~로 제한해 | "Limit it to two times." |

**Variations:**
```
"Twice a week, max."
"Twice a week at most."
"No more than twice a week."
"Twice a week, tops."
"Two times a week, and that's it."
```

### "You can't push yourself too hard."

**Original Korean:** "무리하면 안 돼."

**"Push yourself" Analysis:**
- 스스로를 몰아붙이다
- 운동/공부/일에서 자주 사용

**Effort Expressions Spectrum:**

| Expression | Intensity | Korean |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| "Take it easy." | ★☆☆ | 천천히 해 |
| "Don't overdo it." | ★★☆ | 무리하지 마 |
| "Don't push yourself too hard." | ★★★ | 너무 무리하지 마 |
| "Don't burn yourself out." | ★★★★ | 지치지 않게 해 |
| "Know your limits." | ★★★★ | 한계를 알아 |

**Practice:**
```
A: I'm going to run every day from now on!
B: That's great, but don't push yourself too hard.
A: What do you mean?
B: Start slow. If you overdo it, you might get injured.
A: You're right. Maybe I'll start with three times a week.
B: That sounds more reasonable. Build up gradually.
```

## Pattern 2: "I want to [verb]."

**Structure:** Subject + want + to + verb

**From Text:** "... I want to walk."

| Level | Expression | Nuance |
|-------|------------|--------|

# PART 10: EXTENDED PRACTICE

## 10.1 Dialogue Practice

### Scenario 1: Parent-Child Negotiation

```
Child: Mom, can I walk to school tomorrow?
Mom: I don't know... it's far.
Child: Please? I really want to try.
Mom: [sigh] Okay. But promise me something.
Child: What?
Mom: Eat breakfast before you go. And if it gets too hard,
     catch a bus partway.
Child: Got it. I promise.
Mom: And twice a week max. Don't push yourself too hard.
Child: Okay. Thanks, Mom.
Mom: Be careful.
Child: I will.
```

### Scenario 2: Talking About Progress

```
A: How's the walking going?
B: Good, actually. Today was easier.
A: Really? How so?
B: I wasn't as out of breath. And my legs didn't hurt as much.
A: That's great! You're getting stronger.
B: Maybe. Or maybe it's just the warm weather.
A: Either way, it sounds like progress.
B: Yeah. It's starting to feel... manageable.
```

### Scenario 3: Describing a Walk

```
A: What's it like walking in April?
B: Different from March. It's warmer now.
A: What do you see?
B: Rapeseed flowers everywhere. Both sides of the road
   are yellow. Like waves of gold.
A: That sounds beautiful.
B: It is. I stopped and just took it all in.
A: Did you take any photos?
B: No, I left my phone at home. But I'll remember it.
```

### Scenario 4: Friendly Conversation with a Neighbor

```
Doyun: Good morning!
Old Man: Hey there, student. It's April now.
Doyun: Yes. It's gotten warm.
Old Man: Sure has. Good weather for walking.
Doyun: Are you working again today?
Old Man: Yeah. Harvesting the late tangerines.
         If you get thirsty on your way, have one.
Doyun: Thank you!
Old Man: Go on. You'll be late for school.
```

## 10.4 Discussion Questions

1. **Have you ever had to negotiate with a parent about something you wanted to do?** How did it go? What was the compromise?

2. **How does the weather affect your mood or energy level?** Do you feel different in spring vs. winter?

3. **Have you experienced progress in something over time?** What helped you improve? How did it feel when things got easier?

4. **Do you think "understanding" and "allowing" are the same thing?** Can someone allow something without understanding it?

5. **What's something you appreciate about walking vs. driving/riding?** Have you ever noticed things you wouldn't have seen otherwise?

6. **Describe a time you received a small kindness from a stranger.** How did it make you feel?

7. **The narrator says "Spring doesn't last long."** What does this mean to you? Is there something you appreciate more because it doesn't last?
